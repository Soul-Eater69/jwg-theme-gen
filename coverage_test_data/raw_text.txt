ENGAGEMENT REQUEST: CP 2027 Medicare Advantage Prior Authorization Modernization and Member Experience Uplift

Background and Business Context.
Blue Cross Blue Shield is launching a multi-quarter initiative to modernize the prior authorization (PA) experience across the Medicare Advantage (MA) line of business for plan year 2027. Today, the prior authorization workflow spans Utilization Management nurses, the provider portal, the member portal, the claims adjudication platform, and three downstream vendor integrations covering radiology benefit management, specialty pharmacy, and durable medical equipment. The current process is heavily manual: providers submit PA requests by fax or through a legacy web form, Utilization Management staff key the requests into the clinical review system, and turnaround averages 5.4 business days for standard requests and 28 hours for expedited requests. Members frequently call the contact center to ask about the status of an authorization that affects an upcoming procedure, and the contact center has no real-time view into the authorization queue. This drives avoidable call volume, member abrasion, and provider dissatisfaction, and it creates compliance exposure against CMS timeliness standards for Medicare Advantage organizations. Leadership has identified prior authorization as the single largest source of provider friction and a top-three driver of member complaints in the Medicare Advantage book of business.

Business Problem.
The core problem is that the prior authorization lifecycle is slow, opaque, and disconnected from the systems members and providers actually use. Manual intake introduces transcription errors, duplicate submissions, and rework. There is no automated clinical criteria check at the point of submission, so requests that clearly meet medical necessity criteria still sit in the Utilization Management queue waiting for a human reviewer. Members cannot see authorization status in the member portal, and providers cannot see decision rationale or the specific clinical documentation that is missing when a request is pended. The contact center cannot answer status questions on first contact, which inflates average handle time and repeat-call rate. The lack of structured data capture means the organization cannot report reliably on PA turnaround, approval and denial rates by service category, or appeal overturn rates, which weakens both regulatory reporting and network management. Each of these problems compounds the others: opaque status drives calls, manual intake drives errors, and errors drive pends, appeals, and rework.

Proposed Change and Scope.
This engagement proposes an end-to-end redesign of the Medicare Advantage prior authorization experience with four pillars. First, an intelligent intake capability that accepts structured electronic prior authorization submissions from providers, validates required fields at submission time, and attaches the correct clinical documentation templates by service category. Second, an automated clinical criteria engine that evaluates incoming requests against the plan's medical policy and the applicable InterQual or MCG criteria set, auto-approving requests that clearly meet criteria and routing only the ambiguous or high-cost requests to a Utilization Management nurse. Third, real-time status transparency in both the member portal and the provider portal, including decision rationale, missing-documentation prompts, and estimated decision timelines. Fourth, a contact-center authorization view that surfaces real-time status so agents can resolve status inquiries on first contact. The redesign also includes a structured event model so that every state change in the authorization lifecycle is captured for reporting and audit.

Service Categories In Scope.
The first release targets the highest-volume and highest-friction service categories: advanced imaging including MRI, CT, and PET scans; inpatient elective admissions; skilled nursing facility admissions; home health services; durable medical equipment above a cost threshold; outpatient surgical procedures requiring authorization; specialty pharmacy medications administered in a clinical setting; cardiac procedures; spinal procedures and pain management; and selected genetic and molecular diagnostic tests. Behavioral health inpatient and residential authorizations are in scope for intake and transparency but will continue to route to specialized behavioral health reviewers rather than the automated criteria engine in the first release. Each service category has its own documentation requirements, its own applicable medical policy, and its own criteria set, and the intake capability must present the correct documentation template and required fields for the selected category.

Field-Level Intake Requirements.
Structured intake must capture the member identifier and verify eligibility and benefit coverage at submission; the rendering and ordering provider identifiers and their network status; the requested service or procedure codes; the diagnosis codes supporting medical necessity; the requested units, frequency, and date span; the place of service; and the supporting clinical documentation. The system must validate that required fields are present before accepting the request, must check that the procedure code is one that requires authorization for the member's product, and must detect probable duplicate submissions for the same member, provider, service, and date span. When a provider submits through an electronic health record using the FHIR Prior Authorization Support workflow, the structured data must map cleanly to the internal authorization record without manual re-keying. Intake must also support attachment of imaging orders, clinical notes, lab results, and prior treatment history.

Automated Clinical Criteria Engine.
The criteria engine evaluates each incoming request against machine-readable representations of the plan's medical policy and the licensed InterQual or MCG criteria for the relevant service category. When a request clearly meets criteria based on the structured data and attached documentation, the engine auto-approves the request, records the specific criteria met, the policy version, and the evaluation timestamp, and produces an auditable decision record. When the request is ambiguous, when required documentation is missing, or when the requested service exceeds a defined cost or risk threshold, the engine routes the request to a Utilization Management nurse with the criteria evaluation results pre-populated so the nurse does not start from scratch. The engine must be versioned so that the organization can demonstrate which criteria version was applied to any historical decision, and it must degrade gracefully to manual review for any service category whose criteria are not yet digitized.

Utilization Management Workflow.
Utilization Management nurses require a redesigned worklist that prioritizes expedited requests, flags requests approaching CMS timeliness thresholds, and presents the clinical criteria evaluation results alongside the submitted documentation. The auto-approval path must record the specific criteria met and produce an auditable decision record. Pended requests must generate a structured missing-information request that is visible to the submitting provider with a clear list of what documentation is required and a deadline for response. Denials must capture the specific clinical rationale and the medical policy citation, and must trigger the member and provider denial correspondence with appeal rights language compliant with Medicare Advantage requirements. The system must support peer-to-peer review scheduling when a provider contests a pending denial, and it must allow a medical director to review and finalize adverse determinations. Nurses must be able to request additional information, extend timelines where permitted, and document their clinical reasoning.

Member Populations and Markets.
The initiative targets the Medicare Advantage population across all states where the plan offers MA HMO and MA PPO products, with an initial pilot in two states before national rollout. The change affects approximately 1.2 million MA members, with particular attention to dual-eligible Special Needs Plan members who have higher utilization of services requiring prior authorization. Group retiree MA members are in scope for the transparency features but out of scope for the automated criteria engine in the first release. The commercial and individual lines of business are explicitly out of scope for this engagement, though the platform should be designed so that the criteria engine and intake capability can be extended to commercial prior authorization in a later phase. The pilot markets were selected based on authorization volume, provider readiness for electronic submission, and the availability of digitized criteria for the in-scope service categories.

Stakeholders.
Primary business stakeholders include the Vice President of Medicare Clinical Operations, the Director of Utilization Management, the Medicare Advantage Product Owner, the Member Experience leadership team, the Provider Network Management organization, and the Compliance and Regulatory Affairs office responsible for CMS reporting. Technical stakeholders include the Enterprise Integration team that owns the FHIR and X12 transaction gateways, the Claims Platform team, the Identity and Access Management team that governs provider portal access, the Data and Analytics organization, and the Information Security office. External stakeholders include the three benefit management vendors, the electronic health record vendors whose providers will submit electronic prior authorization transactions, and the CMS-facing regulatory reporting function. Each stakeholder group has signed off on the problem statement, and a steering committee has been established to govern scope and resolve cross-team dependencies.

Systems and Products Impacted.
The change touches the provider portal, the member portal, the clinical review and Utilization Management system, the core claims adjudication platform, the enterprise master member index, the provider directory, the medical policy repository, the InterQual or MCG criteria service, and the enterprise notification service that sends member letters and provider correspondence. Integration points include the X12 278 prior authorization request and response transaction set, the HL7 FHIR Prior Authorization Support implementation guide, the document management system that stores clinical attachments, and the data warehouse that feeds regulatory and operational reporting. The contact center agent desktop and the interactive voice response system are also in scope for the status-transparency features. The eligibility and benefits service must be callable at intake to confirm coverage, and the correspondence platform must generate compliant member and provider letters.

Integration and Transaction Detail.
Providers will submit through three channels: the provider portal web form, the X12 278 transaction for trading partners using clearinghouses, and the HL7 FHIR Prior Authorization Support workflow for electronic health record integrations. All three channels must converge on a single internal authorization record so that downstream processing, reporting, and correspondence are channel-agnostic. The X12 278 response must return the authorization status, reference number, and decision detail. The FHIR workflow must support the Coverage Requirements Discovery and Documentation Templates and Rules components so that an electronic health record can determine in advance whether authorization is required and what documentation is needed. The vendor integrations for radiology, specialty pharmacy, and durable medical equipment must be able to receive the structured authorization request and return their determination, and their decisions must be reconciled into the internal record.

Operational Correspondence and Notifications.
Every authorization decision must drive the appropriate correspondence. Approvals generate a provider notification and, where required, a member notification. Pends generate a structured missing-information request to the provider. Denials generate member and provider adverse determination letters that include the clinical rationale, the medical policy citation, and Medicare Advantage appeal rights and timeframes. All member correspondence must meet Medicare communication and language access requirements, including availability in required threshold languages and accessible formats. The notification service must support both electronic delivery through the portals and printed mail where required, and it must record proof of generation and delivery for compliance. Members should be able to opt into electronic notifications and receive alerts when the status of a relevant authorization changes.

Reporting and Analytics Requirements.
The organization needs operational dashboards covering prior authorization volume by service category, turnaround time against CMS standards, auto-approval rate, nurse-touch rate, denial rate, appeal volume, and appeal overturn rate, all sliceable by market, product, and provider group. Regulatory reporting must support the CMS Medicare Advantage timeliness and organization determination reporting requirements. The data model must capture every state transition in the authorization lifecycle with timestamps so that turnaround can be measured precisely from receipt to decision. Analytics must be able to identify providers with high pend rates due to incomplete submissions so that Provider Network Management can target education, and to identify service categories with high denial or overturn rates so that medical policy and criteria can be refined. Executive reporting must show progress against the program's success measures.

Compliance and Regulatory Considerations.
Because this is a Medicare Advantage program, the design must comply with CMS organization determination timeframes, the recent CMS interoperability and prior authorization final rule provisions affecting Medicare Advantage organizations, and the applicable appeal and grievance requirements. All member-facing correspondence must meet Medicare marketing and communication requirements and language access requirements. Protected health information must be handled in accordance with HIPAA, and the document management of clinical attachments must enforce minimum-necessary access controls. Audit logging must be sufficient to support both internal compliance monitoring and external CMS audit. The criteria engine's versioning must allow the organization to reproduce exactly which criteria were applied to any historical determination, and adverse determinations must follow the required clinical review and medical director sign-off process.

Information Security and Access.
Provider portal access must be governed at the practice and individual provider level, with appropriate identity proofing and role-based access so that a provider sees only the authorizations for members they are treating. Member portal access must follow the existing member identity and authentication standards. All data in transit and at rest must be encrypted, and access to clinical attachments must enforce minimum-necessary principles. The system must produce a complete audit trail of who viewed or acted on each authorization. Service accounts used for the vendor and gateway integrations must follow least-privilege principles, and secrets must be managed through the enterprise secrets management capability rather than embedded in configuration.

Member and Provider Digital Experience.
In the member portal, members should be able to see the status of authorizations that affect their care, understand in plain language what a pended or denied status means, and know the expected decision timeline. In the provider portal, providers should be able to submit electronic prior authorization requests, see real-time status, respond to missing-information requests by uploading documentation directly, and view decision rationale. The experience should reduce the need for providers and members to call the contact center, and where calls do occur, the agent should have a unified real-time view. The digital experience must be accessible in accordance with applicable accessibility standards and must function on mobile devices for both members and providers. The provider experience should also surface, before submission, whether a given service requires authorization for the member's product.

Contact Center Experience.
Contact center agents need a real-time authorization view integrated into the agent desktop so that they can answer a member's or provider's status question on first contact without transferring the call or submitting a research request. The view must show the current status, the decision or the reason for a pend, the expected timeline, and any outstanding documentation requirements. The interactive voice response system should be able to provide basic status information for authenticated callers to deflect simple status calls entirely. Agents must be able to see the full authorization history for a member when appropriate, and the system must respect the same minimum-necessary and identity-verification requirements that govern the portals.

Dependencies and Assumptions.
The initiative assumes that the enterprise FHIR gateway will be available to support the HL7 FHIR Prior Authorization Support transactions and that the X12 gateway can be extended for the new structured intake. It assumes the medical policy repository can expose machine-readable criteria for the highest-volume service categories in the first release. It depends on the Identity and Access Management team to provision provider portal access at the practice and individual provider level. It assumes the three benefit management vendors can consume and respond to the new structured authorization requests. It depends on the Data and Analytics team to build the lifecycle event model and the reporting layer. A key risk is that machine-readable clinical criteria may not be available for all service categories, in which case those categories will continue to route to manual review until criteria are digitized.

Risks and Mitigations.
The primary risks are the availability of digitized clinical criteria, provider readiness for electronic submission, vendor integration timelines, and the regulatory deadline associated with the CMS interoperability and prior authorization rule. To mitigate the criteria risk, the program will sequence service categories by criteria readiness and fall back to manual review where criteria are not digitized. To mitigate provider readiness, Provider Network Management will run an education and onboarding campaign and the portal will remain available for providers not yet integrated through their electronic health record. To mitigate vendor timelines, integration work with the three benefit management vendors will start early and proceed in parallel. To mitigate the regulatory deadline, the transparency and timeliness features that directly address the rule will be prioritized in the first release.

Expected Benefits and Success Measures.
The expected benefits include a reduction in average standard prior authorization turnaround from 5.4 business days to under 2 business days, an increase in the auto-approval rate for clearly-meets-criteria requests to at least 35 percent, a reduction in prior-authorization-related contact center calls by at least 25 percent, and improved CMS timeliness compliance. Provider satisfaction with the authorization process should improve measurably, and the organization should gain reliable, structured reporting on the full authorization lifecycle. Success will be measured by turnaround time, auto-approval rate, contact center call deflection, first-contact resolution for status inquiries, appeal overturn rate, and CMS timeliness adherence. The program will report these measures monthly to the steering committee and quarterly to executive leadership, and will use them to decide when to expand from the pilot markets to national rollout and when to extend the platform to additional service categories and lines of business.

Appendix A: Authorization Lifecycle States.
The structured event model defines the following authorization lifecycle states, each of which generates a timestamped event for reporting and audit: received, eligibility-verified, duplicate-flagged, criteria-evaluating, auto-approved, routed-to-nurse, pended-for-information, information-received, peer-to-peer-scheduled, medical-director-review, approved, partially-approved, denied, withdrawn-by-provider, and expired. Each transition records the actor, whether human or system, the timestamp, the criteria version where applicable, and the rationale. Turnaround is measured from the received event to the terminal decision event, with expedited and standard requests measured against their respective CMS timeliness thresholds. The model must support reopening a decision when a provider submits additional information after a denial within the allowed window, and must link an appeal to its originating determination so that overturn rates can be computed.

Appendix B: Data Dictionary Highlights.
Key data elements captured for each authorization include the authorization reference number, the member identifier and product, the ordering and rendering provider identifiers and network status, the requested procedure and diagnosis codes, the requested units and date span, the place of service, the service category, the channel of submission, the criteria set and version applied, the decision and decision rationale, the medical policy citation, the reviewing nurse and medical director where applicable, the correspondence generated, and the full set of lifecycle event timestamps. Attachments are stored with metadata indicating document type, source, and the date received. Personally identifiable and protected health information elements are tagged so that access controls and minimum-necessary rules can be enforced consistently across the portals, the agent desktop, and the reporting layer.

Appendix C: Representative Scenarios.
Scenario one: a provider submits an advanced imaging request through their electronic health record using the FHIR workflow; the structured data and attached imaging order clearly meet criteria; the engine auto-approves within seconds and returns the authorization reference to the electronic health record and the provider portal, and the member can see the approved status in the member portal. Scenario two: a skilled nursing facility admission request is missing the required clinical assessment; the system pends the request, sends a structured missing-information request to the provider, and the provider uploads the assessment through the portal, after which a nurse completes the review. Scenario three: a high-cost spinal procedure does not clearly meet criteria; the engine routes it to a nurse with the criteria evaluation pre-populated; the nurse pends for additional documentation, schedules a peer-to-peer review at the provider's request, and a medical director ultimately issues a partial approval with documented rationale and compliant correspondence.

Appendix D: Glossary.
Prior authorization is the process by which the plan reviews a requested service for medical necessity and benefit coverage before the service is rendered. An organization determination is the Medicare Advantage term for the plan's decision on whether to provide or pay for a service. Utilization Management is the clinical function that performs medical necessity review. InterQual and MCG are widely used clinical criteria sets licensed by health plans. The X12 278 is the standard electronic transaction for requesting and responding to prior authorization. FHIR Prior Authorization Support is the HL7 implementation guide that enables electronic health records to determine authorization requirements, gather required documentation, and submit requests. A dual-eligible Special Needs Plan serves members eligible for both Medicare and Medicaid. Expedited review applies when the standard timeframe could seriously jeopardize the member's life, health, or ability to regain maximum function.

Appendix E: Rollout Phases.
Phase one delivers the structured intake, the transparency features in the member and provider portals, the contact center authorization view, and the lifecycle event model and reporting foundation, for the two pilot markets and the highest-volume imaging and inpatient service categories. Phase two adds the automated criteria engine for the service categories with digitized criteria, the X12 278 and FHIR channels, and the vendor integrations for radiology, specialty pharmacy, and durable medical equipment. Phase three expands to national rollout across all Medicare Advantage markets, adds the remaining in-scope service categories as their criteria are digitized, and hardens the reporting and regulatory outputs. A later phase, outside the scope of this engagement, would extend the intake capability and criteria engine to the commercial line of business. Each phase has explicit entry and exit criteria tied to the program's success measures, and the steering committee must approve progression between phases.

Appendix F: Non-Functional Requirements.
The intake and status services must support the peak submission volume expected during the annual enrollment and plan-year-start periods without degradation, and the criteria engine must return auto-approval decisions for clearly-meets-criteria requests within seconds so that providers receive near-real-time responses. The portals and the agent desktop must present status without perceptible delay. The system must be resilient to the temporary unavailability of a downstream vendor or gateway by queuing requests and reconciling once the dependency recovers, and it must never lose a submitted request. Availability targets must meet the enterprise standard for member- and provider-facing systems. All services must emit structured logs and metrics sufficient for operational monitoring and for measuring the program's success measures, and the lifecycle event stream must be durable so that no state transition is lost.

Appendix G: Appeals and Grievances.
When a member or provider disputes an adverse organization determination, the appeal must be linked to its originating authorization so that the organization can measure appeal volume and overturn rate by service category, market, and provider group. The appeal intake must capture the basis of the appeal and any newly submitted clinical documentation, route the appeal to a reviewer who was not involved in the original determination, and meet the Medicare Advantage appeal timeframes for standard and expedited appeals. The system must generate compliant acknowledgment and resolution correspondence, must support escalation to an independent review entity where required, and must record the final disposition. Appeal outcomes that overturn an original denial must feed back into criteria and medical policy refinement so that recurring overturn patterns drive improvements to the automated criteria engine and to provider education.

Appendix H: Provider Onboarding and Education.
Provider Network Management will run a structured onboarding program so that practices and facilities can begin submitting electronic prior authorization requests through the portal or their electronic health record. Onboarding includes identity proofing, portal access provisioning at the practice and individual provider level, training on the structured intake and documentation templates, and guidance on which services require authorization for each product. Education materials will explain how to respond to missing-information requests, how to view decision rationale, and how to request a peer-to-peer review. The program will prioritize high-volume practices and facilities in the pilot markets and will track adoption so that the organization can measure the shift from fax and legacy web submissions to structured electronic submissions over time.

Appendix I: Member Communications.
Member communications must explain prior authorization status in plain, accessible language. When an authorization affecting a member's care is approved, pended, or denied, the member should be able to understand what the status means for their upcoming care and what, if anything, they need to do. Denial communications must clearly state the reason, the clinical basis, and the member's appeal rights and timeframes, and must be available in the required threshold languages and in accessible formats. Members who opt into electronic notifications should receive timely alerts when the status of a relevant authorization changes, with a link to the member portal for detail. All member communications must comply with Medicare communication requirements and must avoid clinical jargon that a member would not be expected to understand.

Appendix J: Change Management and Training.
The redesigned Utilization Management worklist, the agent desktop authorization view, and the new intake and criteria capabilities require coordinated change management. Utilization Management nurses must be trained on the new worklist, the criteria evaluation results, the structured missing-information workflow, and the peer-to-peer and medical director processes. Contact center agents must be trained on the real-time authorization view and on how to answer status inquiries on first contact. Provider-facing and member-facing support teams must understand the new transparency features so they can guide users. Training must be delivered ahead of each rollout phase, and the program must maintain updated job aids and knowledge base articles. Adoption and proficiency will be monitored, and additional coaching will be provided where the new workflows are not being used as intended.

Appendix K: Measurement and Governance.
The program establishes a measurement framework tied directly to the success measures: standard and expedited turnaround time against CMS thresholds, auto-approval rate, nurse-touch rate, denial rate, appeal volume and overturn rate, contact center call deflection, first-contact resolution for status inquiries, and electronic submission adoption. These measures are reported monthly to the steering committee and quarterly to executive leadership. Governance includes a steering committee that approves scope changes and phase progression, a clinical governance function that owns medical policy and criteria changes, and a technical governance function that manages cross-team dependencies and integration sequencing. Decisions to expand from pilot markets to national rollout, and to add service categories or lines of business, are made against the measurement framework and the documented phase entry and exit criteria.

Appendix L: Vendor Integration Detail.
The radiology benefit management vendor must receive the structured advanced imaging request, apply its own clinical decision support, and return a determination that is reconciled into the internal authorization record so that the member, provider, and reporting layer see a single consistent status. The specialty pharmacy vendor must receive requests for clinician-administered specialty medications, validate site-of-care and benefit rules, and return its determination with rationale. The durable medical equipment vendor must receive requests above the cost threshold and return its determination. For each vendor, the integration must define the request and response contract, the reconciliation rules when the vendor determination differs from an internal evaluation, the handling of vendor unavailability, and the audit record of which entity made each decision. Vendor service levels for response time must be defined so that vendor latency does not jeopardize CMS timeliness compliance.

Appendix M: Migration and Coexistence.
During rollout, the new structured intake and the legacy fax and web-form intake must coexist. Authorizations created in the legacy process must remain visible and actionable, and providers not yet onboarded to electronic submission must continue to be served without disruption. The migration approach favors routing new submissions for in-scope service categories and pilot markets through the new capability while leaving everything else on the existing process, then progressively expanding as service categories and markets are enabled. Historical authorization data needed for reporting continuity must be mapped into the new lifecycle event model where feasible so that trend reporting spans the transition. No in-flight authorization may be lost or stalled during cutover, and a rollback path must exist for each phase.
